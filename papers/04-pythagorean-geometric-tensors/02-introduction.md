## 1. Motivation and Historical Context

The Ancient Greeks discovered that with merely a compass and straightedge, one could construct an infinite variety of geometric figures. Euclid's *Elements*, compiled around 300 BCE, systematized this constructive approach into thirteen books of propositions, each built upon a foundation of just five postulates. Yet certain problems remained impossible within this axiomatic system: trisecting an arbitrary angle, doubling the cube, and squaring the circle. These limitations were not failures of technique but fundamental boundaries of what can be computed through compass and straightedge constructions alone.

Modern computational geometry has largely abandoned these constructive constraints in favor of analytical methods, trading the elegance of geometric reasoning for the power of algebraic manipulation. A typical computer graphics pipeline evaluates millions of trigonometric functions per frame: `sin(theta)`, `cos(theta)`, `atan2(y,x)`, and their inverse counterparts. Each evaluation involves floating-point approximation of transcendental functions, accumulating numerical errors and consuming computational resources.

**The central question of this dissertation**: Can we recover the exactness and elegance of compass and straightedge constructions while maintaining the computational efficiency of modern tensor algebra?

Pythagorean Geometric Tensors (PGT) answers this question affirmatively by demonstrating that the mathematical essence of geometric constructions can be encoded directly into tensor coefficients, preserving constructive interpretation while achieving $O(1)$ complexity for operations that traditionally require $O(n^3)$ matrix computations.

## 2. The Compass and Straightedge Computational Model

### 2.1 Euclidean Constructions as Algorithms

A compass and straightedge construction proceeds through a sequence of discrete operations:

1. **Circle Drawing**: Given center $P$ and radius $r$, draw all points at distance $r$ from $P$
2. **Line Drawing**: Given two points $P$ and $Q$, draw the unique line through both
3. **Intersection Finding**: Given two circles, a circle and a line, or two lines, find their intersection points

Each operation creates new points that serve as inputs for subsequent operations. This flow of information---points generating circles generating intersections generating new points---suggests a natural algebraic structure waiting to be formalized.

**Example**: Consider erecting a perpendicular to line $L$ at point $P$:

1. Draw circle $C_1$ centered at $P$ with any radius, intersecting $L$ at $A$ and $B$
2. Draw circle $C_2$ centered at $A$ with radius $|AB|$
3. Draw circle $C_3$ centered at $B$ with radius $|AB|$
4. Circles $C_2$ and $C_3$ intersect at points $Q$ and $R$
5. Line through $Q$ and $R$ is perpendicular to $L$ at $P$

This construction encodes not just the final perpendicular line but the entire geometric reasoning process. In PGT, we capture this process as a tensor contraction where each step corresponds to a specific algebraic operation on tensor indices.

### 2.2 The Algebra of Constructions

The key insight is that compass and straightedge constructions operate on *relative* geometric relationships, not absolute coordinates. When we draw a circle, we specify a center and a radius---both relative quantities. When we find intersections, we solve geometric constraints, not coordinate equations.

This relative nature suggests an algebraic representation:

$$\text{Construction} = \bigotimes_{i} \text{Operation}_i$$

where each operation is a tensor that transforms the geometric state. The tensor product composition mirrors the sequential nature of construction steps.

## 3. The Pythagorean Insight

### 3.1 Primitive Pythagorean Triples

The Pythagorean theorem $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ relates the three sides of a right triangle through a simple algebraic identity. For integer solutions---primitive Pythagorean triples like $(3,4,5)$ or $(5,12,13)$---this identity reveals deep connections between number theory and geometry.

**Definition 3.1 (Primitive Pythagorean Triple)**: A triple of positive integers $(a,b,c)$ is a primitive Pythagorean triple if:
- $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$
- $\gcd(a,b,c) = 1$
- Exactly one of $a,b$ is even

All primitive Pythagorean triples are generated by the parametrization:
$$a = m^2 - n^2, \quad b = 2mn, \quad c = m^2 + n^2$$

where $m > n > 0$ are coprime integers of opposite parity.

### 3.2 Pythagorean Angles as Basis Elements

The angles in Pythagorean triangles possess special algebraic properties. For the $(3,4,5)$ triangle:
- $\cos(\theta_1) = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$ (rational!)
- $\sin(\theta_1) = \frac{3}{5} = 0.6$ (rational!)
- $\theta_1 \approx 36.87°$

**Key Observation**: These angles appear irrational when measured in degrees, but their trigonometric functions are *rational ratios*. In PGT, we work directly with these rational ratios rather than their transcendental angular measures, preserving exact arithmetic throughout geometric computations.

### 3.3 The Rational Angle Table

| Triple | $\cos(\theta)$ | $\sin(\theta)$ | Angle (approx) |
|--------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| $(3,4,5)$ | $4/5 = 0.8$ | $3/5 = 0.6$ | $36.87°$ |

This table reveals that Pythagorean angles form a discrete basis for continuous angle space. By combining angles through addition formulas, we can approximate any angle to arbitrary precision.

## 4. From Euclid to Tensors: The Theoretical Bridge

### 4.1 Traditional Tensor Geometry

Traditional tensor algebra provides powerful tools for representing geometric transformations. A rotation matrix in 2D:

$$R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

can rotate any vector by angle $\theta$. However, constructing a specific angle requires calculating trigonometric functions---precisely the kind of transcendental operation that Euclid's methods avoid.

### 4.2 The PGT Approach

PGT bridges this gap by embedding constructive geometric operations within tensor algebra. Rather than representing angles through trigonometric functions, we represent them through Pythagorean ratios that maintain exact arithmetic throughout computations.

For a Pythagorean angle $\theta$ with $\tan(\theta) = a/b$:
$$R_\theta = \frac{1}{c}\begin{pmatrix} b & -a \\ a & b \end{pmatrix}$$

where $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$. This matrix satisfies all rotation properties while using only rational arithmetic (given $a, b, c$ as integers).

### 4.3 Connection to Geometric Algebra

Geometric algebra, pioneered by Grassmann and Clifford in the 19th century, provides a unified framework for geometric computation. The Clifford algebra $\mathcal{C}\ell_{2,0}(\mathbb{R})$ over 2D Euclidean space contains:

- Scalars (grade 0): $1$
- Vectors (grade 1): $e_1, e_2$
- Bivectors (grade 2): $e_1 \wedge e_2 = I$ (pseudoscalar)

Rotations are represented by rotors:
$$R = \cos(\theta/2) + I\sin(\theta/2)$$

**Connection to PGT**: Pythagorean angles provide rational rotor coefficients:
$$R_{3,4,5} = \frac{4}{5} + I\frac{3}{5}$$

This establishes PGT as a discrete subalgebra of the full geometric algebra, where all rotor coefficients are rational.

### 4.4 Connection to Clifford Algebra

In Clifford algebra notation, the geometric product combines the inner and outer products:
$$ab = a \cdot b + a \wedge b$$

For orthogonal Pythagorean basis vectors $u = (b/c, 0)$ and $v = (0, a/c)$:
$$uv = 0 + u \wedge v = I \cdot \frac{ab}{c^2}$$

The bivector coefficient $ab/c^2$ is rational for all Pythagorean triples, enabling exact geometric product computation.

## 5. Applications and Motivation

Modern applications demand geometric computation that is simultaneously intuitive, computationally efficient, and mathematically rigorous:

### 5.1 Computer Graphics

Real-time rendering requires millions of geometric transformations per second. Traditional trigonometric evaluations consume significant GPU cycles. PGT enables:
- Exact rotation matrices without transcendental functions
- $O(1)$ angle snapping with bounded error
- 60 FPS rendering with geometric accuracy

### 5.2 Navigation Systems

Maritime and aerial navigation traditionally uses rhumb line calculations assuming locally flat geometry. PGT provides:
- Dead reckoning with exact arithmetic
- Compass course corrections using 3-4-5 triangles
- 0.01° precision without floating-point accumulation

### 5.3 Origami Mathematics

Paper folding naturally produces angles with rational tangents. PGT enables:
- Flat-foldability verification via Kawasaki-Justin theorem
- Crease pattern optimization with exact constraints
- Rigid origami simulation with provable correctness

### 5.4 Spreadsheet-Based AI Systems

Users intuitively understand grid-based geometry. PGT enables:
- Transparent geometric reasoning in cells
- Step-by-step construction auditability
- GPU-accelerated calculations for large datasets
